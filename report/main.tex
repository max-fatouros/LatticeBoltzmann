\begin{abstract}
    \baselineskip 1.75em
    \noindent The Lattice Boltzmann Method (LBM) is a fluid simulation method well suited to study subsonic flow in complex regimes.
    This work presents a multi-threaded implementation of the LBM in two- and three-dimensions in the Julia programming language, and shows the results of fluid flow over various objects.
    We reproduce the specific patterns of turbulence seen behind cylindrical objects knows as K\'arm\'an vortex streets, then we use the model to optimize the angle of attack for the 2D cross-section and 3D model of a wing imported directly from a 3D CAD model.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

The Lattice Boltzmann Method (LBM) is a computational fluid simulation method that discretizes the Boltzmann equations.
It is a method well suited for complex geometries at low (or below) mach number, and it relatively simple to parallelize because of the inherit locality of many of the computations ~\cite{bottcher_computational_2021,arumuga_perumal_review_2015}.

\subsection{Lattice Gas Automata}

Lattice Gas Automata (LGA) were the precursor to LBMs~\cite{sukop_lattice_2006}.

LGA are cellular automata with individual particles on a lattice. These particles move, or \emph{stream}, at discrete velocities in strait lines, until they encounter another particle on the lattice. When two or more particles encounter one another, they \emph{collide}, and based on some rule set, change their velocities.


\subsection{Lattice Boltzmann Method}\label{sec:introduction::lattice-boltzmann-method}
Similar to Lattice Gas Automata, LBMs also have \emph{collision} and \emph{streaming} steps, but theses steps now act on the phase-space densities instead of individual particles.

The phase-space density\footnotemark{} function for our system of particles is defined as $f(\mathbf x, \mathbf v, t)$.
\footnotetext{
  I am refraining from calling $f$ a probability density function, and $F$ a probability mass function, since they can only be normalized properly at fixed slices through time.
}
The number\footnotemark{} of particles with position $\mathbf x_{0}$, velocity $\mathbf v_{0}$, at time $t_{0}$, such that
\begin{equation}
  (\mathbf x, \mathbf v) < (\mathbf x_{0}, \mathbf v_{0}) < (\mathbf x + \delta \mathbf x, \mathbf v + \delta \mathbf v)
\end{equation}
is given by $f(\mathbf x,\mathbf v, t_{0})\delta \mathbf x \delta \mathbf v$.
\footnotetext{
  For any fixed value $t=t_{0}$, $f(\mathbf x, \mathbf v, t_{0})$ can be thought of as either a probability density function, or a particle-number density function.
  The two interpretations can be converted between one another by multiplying $f$ by a constant normalization factor over all parameters.
  This factor would have no effect on the calculations to follow.
}

The LBM discretizes the system to get the discrete density function
\begin{equation}
F(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t) = \int_{\mathbf x - \frac{1}{2}\Delta \mathbf x}^{\mathbf x + \frac{1}{2}\Delta \mathbf x}\int_{\mathbf v - \frac{1}{2}\Delta \mathbf v}^{\mathbf v + \frac{1}{2}\Delta \mathbf v}\int_{\mathbf x - \frac{1}{2}\Delta t}^{\mathbf x + \frac{1}{2}\Delta t} f(\mathbf x', \mathbf v', t') \delta \mathbf x' \delta \mathbf v' \delta t'
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf x$ and $t$ are now defined on a lattice, and $\mathbf v_{i}$ is a set of $M$ discrete velocities that the particles are constrained to have.

$\mathbf x$, $\mathbf v_{i}$ and $t$ are now to be thought of as defining the centre of a bin of particles with bin-volume $\Delta \mathbf x \Delta \mathbf v_{i}\Delta t$.
The number of particles in the bin is then given directly by $F(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t)$.


To calculate the macroscopic observables of interest, we take the moments of the phase-space density
\begin{equation}
  \rho(\mathbf x, t) = \sum_{i} F(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
  \rho(\mathbf x, t)  \mathbf u(\mathbf x, t) = \sum_{i} \mathbf v_{i} F(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t)
\end{equation}

Where $\rho$ is the number of particles at lattice point $(\mathbf x, t)$ the and $\mathbf u$ is the average velocity of the ensemble of particles at lattice point $(\mathbf x, t)$.


We adopt the notation $F_{i} = F(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t)$. At every given point on our lattice, we can compute the equilibrium density function as~\cite{bottcher_computational_2021}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:equilibrium}
  F^{\textnormal{eq}}_{i}
  = w_{i}\rho
  \left[1
    + \frac{3}{c^{2}}\mathbf u \cdot \mathbf v_{i}
    + \frac{9}{2c^{4}}(\mathbf u \cdot \mathbf v_{i})^{2}
    + \frac{3}{2c^{2}}\mathbf u^{2}
  \right]
\end{equation}
The weight term $w_{i}$, and the rest of~\Cref{eq:equilibrium} can be found by an expansion of the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution in Hermite polynomials~\cite{bottcher_computational_2021}. The term $c=\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}$ is the lattice speed.
The speed of sound can be derived from it as $c_{s} = \frac{c}{\sqrt 3}$

We can then \emph{collide} the particles, to move them one step closer to their equilibrium distribution
\begin{equation}
  F^{*}(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t)
  = F(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t)
  + \frac{\Delta t}{\tau}
  \left[
    F^{\textnormal{eq}}(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t)
    - F(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t)
  \right]
\end{equation}
$\tau$ is defined as the characteristic time of the simulation, and is the parameter we vary to influence the viscosity of the model, defined as~\cite{mei_lattice_2000}
\begin{equation}
  \nu = (\tau - 0.5)c_{s}^{2} \Delta t
\end{equation}


In practice, a dimensionless quantity known as the Reynolds number is used instead to quantify the characteristics of the fluid flow, defined as~\cite{bottcher_computational_2021}
\begin{equation}
  Re = \frac{uD}{\nu}
\end{equation}
where $u$ is the velocity of the fluid at the inlet, and $D$ is a ``characteristic length'' of the simulation, which varies upon definition, but in our case it is the width of the lattice in the y-direction (perpendicular to the fluid flow in all our simulations).



After \emph{colliding}, we \emph{stream} the particles, moving the position components of each density bin $F_{i}(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t)$ in the direction of its velocity and stepping forward in time via
\begin{equation}
  F_{i}(\mathbf x + \Delta t\mathbf v_{i}, \mathbf v_{i}, t + \Delta t)
  = F^{*}(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t)
\end{equation}
where $\Delta t \mathbf v_{i}$ must be chosen such that $\mathbf x + \Delta t \mathbf v_{i}$ falls on a lattice site.
In practice, the velocities are often chosen such $\Delta t$ can be set to $\Delta t = n$ with $n = 1$, or more generally with $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{+}$
\subsection{Boundary Conditions}

The possible boundary conditions we consider in our model are
\begin{enumerate}
  \item periodic
  \item bounce-back
  \item no-bounce
\end{enumerate}

These boundary conditions are defined below.

% The edges of the lattice can have either periodic or no-bounce boundary conditions, the objects inserted in the simulation always have bounce-back boundary conditions.

\subsubsection{Periodic}
As usual, periodic boundary conditions can be ensured by taking the new lattice position modulo the number of lattice points.
We can modify the streaming step in each direction j such that
\begin{equation}
  F_{i}\left((x^{(j)} + \Delta t v^{(j)}_{i})\bmod x^{(j)}_{N_{j}}, v^{(j)}_{i}, t + \Delta t\right)
  = F^{*}(x^{(j)}, v^{(j)}_{i}, t)
\end{equation}

where the superscript $(j)$ denotes the dimensions, and the subscript $k$ denotes the lattice index in the $j$ dimensions, and $N_{j}$ is the number of lattice points in the $j$ dimension.



\subsubsection{Bounce-Back}
Bounce-back boundary conditions are used for the surfaces of objects in the simulations, as opposed to the boundary of the lattice itself.

When a phase-space density element streams into a bounce-back boundary, the density element skips the collision step and reverses all of its discrete velocity elements. Therefore, for all $\mathbf x$ in our boundary, the collision step is written as
\begin{equation}
  F^{*}(\mathbf x, \mathbf v_{i}, t)
  = F(\mathbf x, - \mathbf v_{i}, t)
\end{equation}
the remaining collision steps are unchanged.


\subsubsection{No-Bounce}
``No-Bounce'' boundary conditions are used at the boundaries of our lattice, where we want any density-flow to disappear without reentering the other side of the lattice like they do with only the the periodic conditions in place.
These boundary condition can be implemented in addition to and overtop the periodic conditions.

There are some advanced methods to set a boundary to an arbitrary velocity value (including zero), such as the Zou-He boundary conditions~\cite{zou_pressure_1997}
However, in our case we are only concerned with having the flow disappear at the outlet, and so this can effectively be done by setting the derivative of the density function at the outlet to zero.
This is done simply by setting~\cite{mei_lattice_2000}
\begin{align}
  F^{*}_{i}\left(x^{(j)}_{0}, v^{(j)}_{i}, t\right)
  &= F^{*}_{i}\left(x^{(j)}_{1},  v^{(j)}_{i}, t\right) \\
  F^{*}_{i}\left(X^{(j)}_{N_{j}}, v^{(j)}_{i}, t\right)
  &= F^{*}_{i}\left(X^{(j)}_{N_{j} - 1} - 1, v^{(j)}_{i}, t\right)
\end{align}
after the collision step.

\subsection{Force Calculation}
To calculate the forces on an object during the simulation, we use the fact that all the objects in the simulation use bounce back boundary conditions, and therefore all densities are equal to their negative value in the time step after a collision, to compute the force $F$ as~\cite{schalkers_momentum_2024}
\begin{align}
  F &= \sum_{i} \frac{1}{\Delta t} (\rho(\mathbf x, t) \mathbf u(\mathbf x, t) - \rho(\mathbf x + \mathbf v_{i} \Delta t, t+\Delta t) \mathbf u(\mathbf x + \mathbf v_{i}\Delta t, t + \Delta t)) \\
  &= \frac{2\rho(\mathbf x, t)}{\Delta t} \sum_{i}  \mathbf u(\mathbf x, t)
\end{align}

where $\mathbf x$ is taken at all points within the boundary of the object (technically, within the surface of the object, but we assume that all the inner force values cancel out).



\section{Method}
The code for this these models was written in Julia.
The update step is ran with multiple threads, with each thread handling one hyperplane of the lattice for the calculation of $\rho$, $\mathbf u$, $F^{\textnormal{eq}}$, and $F^{*}$.
For the streaming step however, each thread handles streaming in one discrete velocity direction $i$ instead.
The domain decomposition was done in this way so that each thread handles a completely independent part of the lattice, and so that no stitching of domain boundaries was required afterwards.


The number of spatial dimensions $N$ and the number of discrete velocities $M$ are often characterized by a naming scheme as DNQM. Therefore, a system in 2 dimensions with 9 discrete velocities is denoted as D2Q9.

In this paper, the D2Q9 and D3Q15 methods are implemented.

Both methods set $\Delta t = 1 lt$ and $\Delta x = 1 lx$, where, using the unit-notation defined in~\cite{sukop_lattice_2006}, $[lt]$ denotes lattice-time units and $[lx]$ denotes lattice-space units.


To load 2D models into a simulation, we save a drawing of a CAD model at a tiff image (a raw image), and load the image into Julia using the \verb|TiffImages|~\cite{nagy_tlnagytiffimagesjl_2025} package.
To load 3D models into a simulation, we save a CAD model as an STL file, we then convert that to a point cloud (a set of position values) using the \verb|stl-to-voxel| program~\cite{pederkoff_cpederkoffstl--voxel_2025}.
In both cases, we get a point cloud that we can bin to either pixels or voxels for use in our simulation.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Speed of Sound}
As noted in \Cref{sec:introduction::lattice-boltzmann-method}, the speed of sound for the lattice is defined as
\[
  c_{s} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}
\]
Since, for our models, $\Delta x = 1 lx$ and $\Delta t = 1 lt$, then $c_{s} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \frac{lx}{lt} \approx 0.577 \frac{lx}{lt}$.

For our first test, we would like to see if a pulse in the lattice propagates at this speed.
\Cref{fig:speed-fit} shows fits to the peak of a pulse as is travels through the lattice for the 2D and 3D models.
We generate a pulse by adding a source to the model for one update step, then removing it at the next update step and for the rest of the simulation.
The plots show results over 100 time steps, but the numerical results given below were given over 500 time steps.

For the 2D model, $c_{s}$ was found to be
\[
  c_{s} = 0.584 \pm 0.43
\]

And for the 3D model,
\[
  c_{s} = 0.584 \pm 0.42
\]

These both certainly agree within their uncertainties with the set value of $c_{s} \approx 0.577$. However, the uncertainties seem unreasonably large. This seems to be in part due to the discretization error of the positions, as can be seen in~\Cref{fig:speed-fit-2d,fig:speed-fit-3d}.
This discretization error averages out, but will contribute cumulatively to the uncertainty, giving us the acceptable mean, but large uncertainties that we see.
I've left more significant figures than usual in the mean values as a result of this.


\subsection{K\'arm\'an Vortex Streets}
A K\'arm\'an vortex street is a phenomen that can occurs behind objects placed in uniform streams, in which an area of alternating vortices appers~\cite{schatzman_model_1981}.

In~\Cref{fig:vortex-all-2d}, we see the speed, curl, and velocity of fluid producing a K\'arm\'an vortex street behind a disk.
It is clear from the graph of the curl that there is an area or alternating vorticity behind the disk.

\Cref{fig:vortex-speeds-2d} shows how the vortex street behaves at a variety of Reynolds numbers.
As expected, the size and vorticity of the streams is proportional to the Reynolds number.

\subsection{Optimal Wing Angle of Attack}
A wing, such as one on an airplane or an animal, produces lift and drag.
Drag is the force on a wing in the direction of airflow.
Lift is the force on a wing perpendicular to airflow.

Lift can be increased by raising a wing, and therefore increasing the angle of attack between the wing and the direction of travel, but raising a wing also has the effect of increasing drag.
Therefore, it is a non-trivial task to find the optimal angle of attack to maximize the lift over drag ratio.

\Cref{fig:wing} shows various angles of the 3D wing model used in the following simulations.
\begin{figure}[h!]

    }

\end{figure}



\Cref{fig:wing-1} shows the cross-section of a wing at various configurations of the 2D simulation.
The left plot shows the wing with an angle of attack of 15 degrees, which gives a near optimal lift to drag ratio (that we compute below).
The right plot shows the wing at various angles from 0 to 30.
We see that the wing begins to stall past an angle of about 15 degrees, meaning that the flow starts to separate from the wing and cause major turbulance.

To find the optimal angle of attack, also known as the critical angle, we varied the angle from 0 to 30 degrees and ran the simulation for $10^{4}$ time steps.
At each angle we tested, the first half of the time steps were discarded, then at the remaining steps the force was averaged.
From \Cref{fig:wing-2}, we see that the lift to drag ratio peaks for the 2D simulation at 14 degrees, while for the 3D simulation, it peaks at around 24 degrees.
It should be noted the the 3D simulation was ran at half the Reynolds number of the 2D simulation since the 3D simulation was not stable at higher Reynolds number values.
These values are close to the typical critical angle of attack of 15--20 degrees for real planes~\cite{clancy_aerodynamics_1996}.

\section{Conclusion}
In conclusion, we note that the LBM is a powerful tool that can be used to model a variety of fluid simulations in two or three dimensions.
We found that
\begin{itemize}
  \item the measured speed of sound in the lattice agreed with the value it was set to.
  \item The model produces K\'arm\'an vortex streets as expected from a fluid simulation.
  \item The model can be used to optimize the parameter of real world models, similar to how computational fluid dynamic software is used in industry.
\end{itemize}
